Certifications & Terms

What all the terms mean.

SDVOSB stands for Service-Disabled Veteran-Owned Small Business. It means a veteran with a service-connected disability owns most of the company and actually runs it, not just on paper. PFG qualifies under these rules, with certification currently in progress. Read the official rules on the Small Business Administration's website.

Both mean a business is majority owned and controlled by a veteran. SDVOSB specifically requires the veteran to have a service-connected disability, while VOSB does not. PFG qualifies under both categories.

A NAICS code is a number the government uses to sort businesses by what kind of work they do. It helps agencies match a contract to the right company. PFG holds codes for facilities support, construction, freight and hazmat transport, grounds maintenance, moving services, and general support. Look up any code on the U.S. Census Bureau's website.

SAM.gov is the government's official system for registering as a federal contractor. A company has to be listed there before it can bid on or win a federal contract. PFG's registration, along with its UEI and CAGE code, is currently in progress. Visit SAM.gov directly to see how it works.

UEI stands for Unique Entity ID. It is the number every business needs in SAM.gov to be eligible for federal contracts, and it replaced the older DUNS number system. PFG's UEI is part of its SAM.gov registration, currently in progress.

CAGE stands for Commercial and Government Entity code. It is a short code that identifies a specific business location for federal contracting and logistics purposes. PFG's CAGE code is part of its SAM.gov registration, currently in progress.

It is a short summary of what a company does, what it is certified for, and how to reach it. Contracting officers use it to quickly check if a business is a good fit. PFG's capability statement can be viewed online, and the full document is available on request.

A set-aside is a federal contract that only certain types of businesses are allowed to compete for, such as small businesses or SDVOSBs. Set-asides exist so smaller companies are not squeezed out by large corporations on every opportunity. Read more on the Small Business Administration's website.

A prime contractor holds the contract directly with the federal agency and is responsible for the entire scope of work. A subcontractor is hired by the prime to complete part of that work. PFG takes on both roles depending on the opportunity.

Did You Know

A business does not need its own SDVOSB certification to benefit from one. Prime contractors often team with certified small businesses to meet federal set-aside requirements, which is exactly why teaming inquiries matter to PFG.
